Aesthetics and Usability

  • By Hannah Kwan
  • February 26, 2020

In the previous post, we saw that the increased use of aesthetics in human-computer interactions. But do aesthetics have a functional use besides a better appearance? Can aesthetics increase the usability of a product?

In a study done in 2011, Lavie and Tractinsky did a study to explore the relationship between aesthetics and usability. They conducted a couple of experiments to determine if aesthetics affected the usability of an in-vehicle navigation display. Since a navigation display is highly functional, the usability of the device is important. After three separate studies, they concluded that a display with a higher aesthetics score correlated with a higher usability score, and less clutter increased usability.

It may also be that the high usability of the minimal detail maps affected the perceived aesthetics of the maps, and irrelevant information presented on maps made the maps appear less aesthetic.

Lavie and Tractinsky

However, because aesthetics and usability can be subjective, further papers have tried to explore the relationship between them in a more objective way. Tuch et al. wrote a paper asking the question “Is beauty really usable?” The authors’ tried to separate interface-aesthetics from interface-usability as much as possible to produce more objective results. In doing several experiments, the authors’ concluded that the users’ “hedonic quality identification” actually depended less on the perceived aesthetics, but more on perceived usability. This indicated a stronger relationship between the users’ value of usability over aesthetics.

The difficult aspect in the relationship between aesthetics and usability is not only its subjectivity, but also its development over time. As computer screens have gotten more pixels and smartphones have become more popular, aesthetics have changed over the last couple of decades. Some of these studies, completed in the last decade, seem outdated based on its user interfaces and designs. The discrepancy between previous studies and the current state of application aesthetics could be a threat to the validity since the studies do not represent the aesthetics we are used to today. Researchers have tried to explore this difficulty, but between Lavie and Tractinsky and Tuch et al., the relationship between aesthetics and usability is not clearly defined. In fact, as further studies have shown, this relationship varies even more in different cultural contexts. In 2016, Oyibo and Vassileva researched the relationship between aesthetics, usability, and credibility in different cultures. They studied between two groups, one Canadian group, and one Nigerian group. They acknowledged the varying perspectives on the relationship between aesthetics and usability, but in their study they found that, overall, aesthetics has an affect on usability, but to what a degree depends on the culture. This study introduces another aspect on the effect of aesthetics. Oyibo and Vassileva looked how aesthetics and usability affected credibility, both generally and in a cultural context. Similarly to aesthetics and usability, there is an overall correlation between better aesthetics and greater credibility, but culturally, the effect of aesthetics on credibility influenced the Canadian group more than the Nigerian group.

There are other relationships that HCI researchers have studied, one of these being the relationship between aesthetics and credibility. We will explore this briefly in the next post.

I did find some studies that were very specific that were more objective. One group of researchers, Fan, Hu, Zhao and Zhang, from Beijing, China studied the ideal size of two different touch buttons, a round button and a rounded corner button, on a mobile phone. They had thirty participants and they tested different size buttons for each type of button, and recorded reaction times and error rates by having participants correctly type numbers displayed to them. They also had participants rate their satisfaction on a scale from one to five. From these tests, they found that the average reaction time and error rate decreased with the increasing size of the button. They recommend that the best sizes for a round button are between 13 and 19mm and the best sizes for a rounded corner button are between 11 and 19mm. This kind of study helps quantify components of the aesthetics. However, this has limitations as well. There are many different kinds of components that make a user interface, and testing them all would require many studies. At the same time, this does not directly test the relationship between aesthetics and usability, but determines the aesthetics through testing.
