What Are Aesthetics?

  • By Hannah Kwan
  • February 26, 2020

Humans interact with a user-interface that mitigates the interaction between the human and the computer in human-computer interactions. The aesthetics of that user-interface might make or break the user experience. This topic has concerned researchers in the last couple of decades.

So what are aesthetics?

Aesthetics

a particular theory or conception of beauty or art, a particular taste for or approach to what is pleasing to the senses and especially sight

Meriam-Webster

In 2004, Lavie and Tractinsky performed a study on categorizing aesthetics in terms of a users’ perception of a website. This was an early paper in aesthetics in human-computer interaction that sought to define aesthetics in a technological context. As stated in the paper, aesthetics had not been a priority in human-computer interaction in 2003, even though it had some ground in other fields.

In relation to human-computer interaction, aesthetics have become increasingly desirable, since the publishing of the Lavie and Tractinsky paper. Lavie and Tractinsky mention the iMac as a landmark in hardware aesthetics. Once again in 2007, the first iPhone was released, changing the human-computer interaction space forever. As the screen became smaller, the design of the software in mobile devices became much more important.

While interest in all aspects of the human–computer interaction experience is emerging, relatively little is known about users’ preferences outside the realm of efficient task performance.

Lavie and Tractinsky

Lavie and Tractinsky strove to define aspects of aesthetics through several studies. They found that a user’s perceived aesthetics fell under two categories: classical aesthetics and expressive aesthetics. Classical aesthetics are described as “well-organized”, “clear”, “clean” and “symmetrical”, while expressive aesthetics are described as “originality”, “fascinating design” and “using special effects”. The authors stressed classical aesthetics, claimed that “this factor may represent the functionalist ideal as perceived by the users, where beauty is determined by the extent to which form follows function.” Classical aesthetics may have a stronger connection to usability than expressive aesthetics.

This paper was just the beginning of aesthetics in human-computer interaction. The authors point out some factors that challenge the results of the study. They acknowledge that factors such as familiarity with a website, language and culture can affect a user’s perception of aesthetics. We’ll explore these other factors in another post. But for now, we’ll conclude with a quote from Lavie and Tractinsky.

We believe that the scales developed here capture the essence of the visual aesthetic experience in human–computer interaction regardless of the medium used to facilitate the interaction, although the two factor distinction may be more pronounced in certain application genres than in others.

Lavie and Tractinsky

Even in 2003, the authors’ foresaw a categorization of aesthetics helpful to any human-computer interaction, “regardless of the medium used”, before the use of smartphones. We’ll focus on the application of aesthetics to mobile applications.
