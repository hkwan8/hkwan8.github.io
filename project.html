Exploration of Gathered User Data and its Effects on User Interactions in a Goal Achievement Application

Introduction

In today’s current consumer technology market, there are multitudes of applications that rely on user-gathered data in order to drive the experiences of their app. For Cal Poly start up company Trees, the success of their goal-achievement mobile application relies on gathering user data like personality, geographic, and demographic data to create personally-tailored action plans for those seeking to achieve a goal. Due to the sensitive nature of the data being collected, Trees strive to be as transparent as possible to its users about the data the app gathers from them. The goal of this project is to gather data about students’ opinions about data collection and whether data transparency affects their perceptions of an app and to create a prototype for the onboarding process of the Trees app. Our design aims to inform users of the purpose of the app while being upfront about data collection practices and, ultimately, we hope to create an interface that users feel secure and comfortable using.

Overview

A Cal Poly local incubator company is planning to build Trees, a goal-achievement app that aims to generate personally-curated action plans to help users achieve a goal in a way that suits each user. It plans to create these action plans by taking effective user-generated goal-achievement plans and gathering users’ personality, demographic, geographic, and habit data, and incorporating them into a machine learning algorithm that takes these features and generates action plan steps for users. This data will be gathered from questionnaires that users fill out through the app. Generic goal plans often don’t consider variables and availability of resources that can limit one’s ability to accomplish said goal, but by accounting for these factors, Trees hopes that more people will be successful in achieving their goals.

One of the major potential concerns about this app is its usage of user data. Many apps today gather data from users like location, screen time, visited websites, etc. in order to tailor advertisements and other experiences to the user’s interests, but many users are not actually aware of the amount of data that is being gathered. The Trees company is mindful of the fact that the app will require the collection of sensitive user data for the app to recommend action plans properly. Trees hopes to proactively alleviate user concerns about data abuse by being transparent and upfront about what data is being collected by the app.

Initial discussions with the Trees team determined that one way they plan to uphold data transparency is to provide a data dashboard feature within the app. On this page, users would be able to view what data the app has collected along with justifications as to why the collected data is needed by the app and how that information is used. This page would also allow users to edit or delete certain types of collected data. The goal of this dashboard is to reassure users that the Trees application is only collecting necessary data and using it responsibly, hopefully increasing user trust.

However, during the early stages of our design process, the question of whether users would even use the data dashboard came up. Do users care enough about data collection that they would use the data dashboard feature provided? These concerns caused a shift of focus for this project from designing an interface for controlling data collection to designing an onboarding experience for the Trees app that informs users of their data collection means. In addition, through this project, we hope to get a better understanding of users’ opinions on data collection and whether a clear data transparency policy affects their perception and trust in an app. In the end, we aim to create a design that upholds Trees’ goal of data transparency.

Goal-setting is an activity that people of all demographics and ages can partake in, thus the target audience for this app is very broad. For this project, we are focusing on one of the major intended user bases for this app: students. Example goals that this app could help students achieve include earning better grades, becoming more sociable, or learning a new hobby.

Background and Related Work

Prior to this project, Trees has created a number of UI designs for the primary functionalities of the app, ranging from lower fidelity wireframes to higher fidelity prototypes with interactive components. However, there is presently no mockup or wireframe for the onboarding process for a new user. This provides an opportunity to explore methods on how to be transparent about data collection during the introductory phase of an app. To maintain Trees’ goal of data transparency, it is key to design an interface where this information is easily communicated to the user.

While there are companies who are transparent about their data collection practices, most prefer to keep consumers in the dark, choose control over sharing, and ask for forgiveness rather than permission. The companies that are considered transparent traditionally explain the nuances of their data collection practices in a privacy policy page. For example, Google has several web pages explaining the usage of user data across their services, and offers visual tutorials on how to access privacy controls within their suite of applications.

Relevance

UI/UX is a subsection of Human-Computer Interaction that focuses on the interfaces and experiences of computational devices. Analyzing and comparing the different amounts and kinds of interactions across varying designs will help determine pain points, areas with heavy mental load, and other aspects that shape the experience that users have with such a device. It’s important to understand how users interact with a product and interface, as that can determine the retention of regular users and traffic, which drive the fiscal and social success of an application. In particular, the onboarding experience is one of the most important, as it can influence whether a user wants to continue using an app.

Furthermore, transparency about data collection is a relatively new topic in UI/UX, since this phenomenon has rapidly expanded in recent years due to advances in mobile technology and data collection practices. Currently, many companies prefer not being transparent to their users about their data collection. Exploring how to display such complex information and how it affects user perceptions and attitudes can contribute towards the push to get more companies to be more honest to users about this topic.

Experimental Design

Since our project relies on gathering user feedback and data about their stances on certain topics, we created a design for our user testing. First, we created our prototype using Figma, due to its collaboration support and its ability to project the prototype to a mobile device. When conducting user tests, users will interact with the prototype through a mobile device.

First, we need to get an initial impression of their experience and beliefs about data privacy. This will help determine if a typical user is concerned about data collection, and if transparent policies will affect their perception of an application. These questions lend to more open-ended responses.

Some questions that intend to gather user information about this include:

  • Are you aware of what data apps are collecting on you? What do you know about the collection of data in social media and technology?
  • How much do you care about apps taking your data?
  • Do you know what data they collect? What do they use it for?
  • How much would you pay (Amazon, Google, etc) to not collect and sell your data?
  • How would you feel if apps gave you the ability to easily control the data you’re giving to the app? Any particular feelings about this?

As desired by our stakeholder, the Trees company, we also asked questions about how users like to achieve goals. This will give us an understanding of how likely users would use an app like Trees, which is valuable information for our stakeholders.

Some questions that intend to gather user information about this include:

  • How do you usually teach yourself something/where do you go to learn skills?
  • Did you have any New Year’s resolutions? How are they going? What did you do to carry them out?

Users will interact with our Figma prototype with two interviewers. One interviewer will focus on taking notes using a Google form. The other will focus on asking questions. We will encourage the user to comment on their thought process when using the app and ask them to discuss any pain points or frustrations.

Lastly, the interview will conclude by asking users to complete a user survey to help generate more quantifiable data. The questions in this survey would be similar to the questions above, but modified to receive a quantitative answer, by adding “on a scale from 1 to 10…”. In addition, the survey will also ask about users’ comprehension about the Trees app by asking them to briefly explain what Trees does, and ask about their college year and major.

Survey questions include:

  • Can you briefly explain, in your own words, what the app, Trees, is about and what it does?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, how helpful do you think action plans would be to you with achieving goals?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, how likely would you use an app like Trees?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, how pleasant was the on-boarding process for the Trees app?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, how simple was the navigation during the on-boarding process for the Trees app?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, how much do you care about data privacy, data collection, and data transparency in technology, social media, and other computational devices?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, if an app provided a feature that will allow you to control your data / prevent certain data from being used by an app, how likely would you use this feature?
  • On a scale from 1 to 10, how comfortable would you be with allowing an app like Trees to use your data?

First Prototype

For our first iteration, we created a high fidelity prototype of the onboarding process for the Trees mobile application. For our first round of user testing, users will interact with this prototype from a mobile device by clicking buttons and other elements on these screens.

The prototype offers a quick introduction to the app and immediately gets users to start creating action plans for their goals. Following the three introduction screens, users can select a goal they want to achieve.

From the third screen (titled Onboarding 2 in the image above), users have the option of clicking the “demographic”, “geographic”, and “personality” links, that will bring up pop up windows that explain how this data is used by Trees. These pop up screens aim to achieve Trees’ goal of data transparency by clearly and concisely explaining what users’ data will be used for.

Lastly, onboarding proceeds by having users go through and answer a quick sample questionnaire for generating action plans and select action plans. This portion incorporates many existing elements and designs that have already been designed for the Trees app.

Implementation

For our user tests, we outlined two questionnaires in Google Forms, one for the interviewer to fill out during the test, and one for the interviewee to fill out at the end of the user test. These Google Forms contain the questions discussed in our experimental design. In the beginning of our user testing process, we ran two practice tests with a rough draft of the questionnaires. These initial user tests helped us realize any format adjustments and question improvements that needed to be improved. We also made some minor adjustments for the forms for interviewers, like increasing the granularity of the text boxes for inputting answers to questions. We also updated the general questions we ask users, for example, we shortened the number of questions focusing on their knowledge on data collection, in order to expedite the over interview process. In this process, we managed to conduct 5 full formal user tests and interviews, and an additional 2 shorter user tests, totaling in 7 completed user testing sessions.

User Feedback

We conducted interviews with seven people. Five of those people filled out our final survey. The following graphs show the demographics of our users. To protect the confidentiality of our participants, we only collected their year and their major.

The participants had the following majors: Graphic Communication, Kinesiology, Business, Materials Engineering, and Economics.

The following graphs display the responses to each of the questions that asked for a rating between 1 and 10. These two questions asked the user about the usage of the app itself. We found that while users found action plans might be helpful in achieving goals, most of the users would likely not use the Trees app. This suggests that the prototype may be lacking in qualities that can retain and attract users.

The user was then asked two questions related to the UI of the app. The responses were mostly positive, and signified that our prototype successfully conducted a pleasant and usable experience.

The last three questions measured how much users cared about data transparency.

One interesting result was that users did not care much about data transparency and privacy, but when asked if they would use a feature that would allow them to control their privacy, the majority of users said they would use such a feature. This indicates that there is a desire for a feature to control data being collected.

Overall, the response was that users would trust Trees with their data. This suggests that the prototype was successful in evoking trust from users.

Finally, for the responses to the free response survey question “Can you briefly explain, in your own words, what the app, Trees, is about and what it does?,” all were able to correctly identify that Trees was an application that aids in goal achievement and management. This indicates that our first prototype was successful in introducing the app’s purpose to users. However, for that same question, only 40% of the responses mention anything relating to using user data to generate action plans (i.e. mentioning personality, demographics, etc), suggesting that information regarding data collection wasn’t presented well in our prototype.

The phenomena above is likely due to one of the major issues we found in our user testing: no one selected the links on the third page of the onboarding process. These are the links that bring up pop up boxes about information on how Trees use users’ demographic, personality, and geographic data. This is problematic because it means that the app fails to inform users about its data collection practices, thus it is not upholding Trees’ data transparency goals.

To supplement our formal first round of user testing, we conducted one round of A/B testing during our mid-quarter presentation. We showed three versions of a screen, and asked people in passing to draw a tally on the screen that would encourage them to click on the links. The votes were unanimous for the first option: selecting a screen that highlighted the options through a lightbox overlay.

Another frustration that users had during user testing was the inability to click every element on the prototype. Because our prototype was designed in Figma, it was not fully functional, and users struggled with broken links, and animations that did not fully simulate the end-product. This is just a consequence of the nature of prototypes/mockups, and the only way to improve this is to add more screens and functionality to our prototypes.

Overall, users reacted positively with the first prototype, and found the onboarding process to be pleasant. However, several users expressed that they will likely not use an app like Trees. They did have suggestions on how to improve the app in a way that could help them want to use the app more often. One particular concern that a user had was that, visually, Trees did not immediately present itself as a goal achievement app from the very first screen--users wanted to know what the app is about from the very first screen. Several users suggested adding animations/graphics on the first page to help with this. Another user also suggested adding user “success stories” to make the action plan and app more credible and reliable. A user also stated that implementing to-do lists or integration with Cal Poly study sessions and services would get him to use the app more. A few also noted that the onboarding process overall felt a bit long, especially with the inclusion of a full length questionnaire.

Second Prototype

Following the results of our user testing, we iterated and improved upon our first prototype based on user feedback.

First, we added a tree graphic in the welcome page. In doing so, there is more variety in the page, and provides something for users to associate the Trees app with. The current graphic is an open-source placeholder and would ideally be replaced by a proper logo for Trees. Then, we added a light box overlay to indicate that the “demographic”, “geographic”, and “personality” text are clickable—a direct result of the feedback we got from our AB test. With this, we hope that more users would be inclined to click the text and learn more about how Trees use their data.

Lastly, we added a user testimony section where users select a goal they want to achieve. This section would contain real users’ testimonies explaining how the action plan they received helped them successfully achieve the goal that the user selected. By adding this, we hope that users feel that the app is more personable, relatable, and credible, which could inspire users to use the app more frequently.

We also took the user feedback, particularly the feedback on the app’s credibility and the onboarding process’s length and ability to present what the app is about on the first screen, to create an alternate version of the welcome screen. In this take, the very first screen includes a description of what Trees does, as well as a punchy app subtitle and images of a person performing an action/goal that can be achieved through this app. There are four description screens in total, in which users navigate through by sliding, but sliding to the other description screens are optional--users can simply tap “Get Started” and immediately go to the goal selection screen.

This design offers a more streamlined onboarding process as users no longer have to go through all of the information screens in the first prototype. Furthermore, adding images of real people and a memorable catchphrase makes the app appear more credible, personable, and relatable. The downside is that, because the description screens are now optional to look through, users are likely not going to read the other screens, including information about data collection. Also, because of the streamlined nature of this version, there is less room to present information about how user data is used. This version essentially sacrifices Trees’ goal of being upfront about data collection and usage for retaining and attracting users.

Conclusions

Coming into this project, we were excited to make a user interface that had real-world applications, especially in the world of data transparency. It was helpful to have meetings roughly once a week with our stakeholder to lay down requirements and discuss our prototype.

One valuable experience we gained from this project was navigating the intricacies of working with an external stakeholder. Balancing company needs and expectations with our academic goals was an interesting challenge. In this process, we discovered the importance of defining requirements before prototyping and performing experimental design to plan user tests that gather the data we are seeking for. Knowing these items, we were able to design specific features to meet the needs of the company and the user. We also learned about the significance of stakeholders’ intellectual property, and how to properly incorporate it into our designs while preserving its confidentiality.

In regards to data transparency, it was interesting to see that many of the college-aged students who participated in our user testing didn’t care too much about data transparency, but would control what data they share when presented. We think this phenomena is a result of companies’ current approach of being discreet about their data collection. Because people aren’t actively thinking about responsible data collection in their experiences with apps, it’s not something they end up caring about.

Lastly, this project highlighted the significance of understanding the audience of the product and designing with the audience in mind. When we had initially planned to design the data control interface, we weren’t sure if users would actually use a feature like this. But after going through this project—designing the onboarding and asking about users’ impressions on data transparency—we were able to gather some desire for this functionality.

For the future, we hope that our work will help Trees make informed design decisions and that our prototype can serve as helpful foundations leading to their final application design. The next step would be to conduct more user testing and iterate upon our onboarding interface more, as well as design the data dashboard feature.
