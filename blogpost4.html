Design Guidelines

  • By Hannah Kwan
  • March 10, 2020

The most popular companies have design guidelines for developers to follow to make their applications consistent in the application store. The existence of these guidelines demonstrate the importance of aesthetics to companies like Apple, Google, and even Microsoft. They have elaborate, user-friendly, and impressively designed websites to reflect the quality they expect in developers to match their platform.

A Comparison of Apple and Google Design Guidelines

Both Apple and Google have online design guidelines. Though the applications made for these companies are on different platforms, the design guidelines share many similarities.

The Website

Apple named their guidelines the Human Interface Guidelines. Outlined are the rules the Apple uses for its own products that they share with developers. One of Apple’s design principles is aesthetic integrity, seeming to agree that aesthetics do affect usability.

Aesthetic integrity represents how well an app’s appearance and behavior integrate with its function. For example, an app that helps people perform a serious task can keep them focused by using subtle, unobtrusive graphics, standard controls, and predictable behaviors.

Apple

There are different categories, such as, User Interaction, Visual Design and specific functional components like Views and Controls.

Google has guidelines named Material Design. One of Material Design’s main principles is the thought that “Material is the metaphor”.

Material Design is inspired by the physical world and its textures, including how they reflect light and cast shadows. Material surfaces reimagine the mediums of paper and ink.

Material Design

In their principles, they claim that Material Design is “guided by print design methods” to make its interface familiar to users. There are two main sections, one tab for design, such as color, typography and layout, and another tab for components like Buttons, Menus and Text fields. Apple has different design guidelines for each of their systems, like macOS and iOS, while Google has general design guidelines.

Recommendations

Both guidelines make recommendations in each section, even displaying side-by-side comparisons for do’s and don’t’s, as well as general recommendations for each topic. For example, here are some screenshots of both Apple’s and Google’s recommendation for margins.

Colors are used for emphasis, such as red for an incorrect practice and green for a good practice.

Why Design Guidelines?

Apple and Google are not the only companies with design guidelines. Microsoft has design guidelines named Fluent Design, Samsung has design guidelines by the name One UI, and even Amazon has design guidelines for the Amazon Fire TV. The main reason for these guidelines is to create a unified experience for users on the specific platform. These guidelines have become necessary because many developers can create applications and put them in any application store.

Due to the widespread diffusion of mobile devices, consumers expect user-friendly and well-designed mobile applications from service providers in various industries.

Hoehle and Venkatesh

Hartmut Hoehle studied how effect following design guidelines would impact both “continued intention to use” and “mobile application loyalty” with Apple and Microsoft guidelines. Hoehle, Aljafari, and Venkatesh conducted a study on Microsoft guidelines by evaluating ten constructs: aesthetic graphics, color, control obviousness, entry point, fingertip-size controls, font, gestalt, hierarchy, subtle animation and transition. They conducted a survey with 550 German consumers and found that these constructs based on Microsoft guidelines did lead users to continue to use the application and brand loyalty. Hoehle and Venkatesh proved the same with Apple’s guidelines, but using 19 constructs from the guidelines and on 1,200 U.S. consumers. These studies demonstrate the value of design guidelines. There is some evidence that the constructs contained in company design guidelines do positively impact the consumer and is economically beneficial since it leads to continued use and brand loyalty. As they indicated in the paper, consumers now have certain expectations from mobile applications, and one way to meet those expectations is to use a design similar to platforms they are using.

Users are more likely to use something that is familiar to them, such as a design similar to the operating system they have. This is supported by a study done in 2019 by Bhandari, Chang, and Neben, “Understanding the impact of perceived visual aesthetics on user evaluations: An emotional perspective”. They manipulated the classical and expressive aesthetics of a mobile app to assess which elicited a stronger emotional response, which was measured in valence, how positive the experience was for the user, and arousal, the “activation of particular emotions”. Then, they used these metrics to determine how it affected the participant’s judgement of the application.

These researchers emphasize the importance of mobile application aesthetics:

The importance of mobile platform is undeniable. Moving forward, user experience is going to become key to differentiating brands/platform owner and for users to associate with. In this light, this study was able to show a process-oriented approach to understand what could be the potential reasoning behind users choosing certain apps compared to other apps based on aesthetic evaluation and “affective” responses.

Bhandari, Chang, and Neben

This study demonstrated the importance of emotions in a user’s decision-making. These emotions are more effected by classical aesthetics than expressive aesthetics. By using these design guidelines, developers increase the chance that the users will make positive decisions in considering the application. And as time has shown, mobile phones have become the norm, and designing the interfaces properly will benefit both the developer and the user.
